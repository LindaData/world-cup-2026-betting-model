Methodology

How The Forecast Works

The site combines historical international results, team-strength features, recent form, venue context, weather, and live football feeds where available. The public forecast is an ensemble of a result model and a score-grid model. In knockout matches, the draw-after-regulation probability is converted into advance probability because the match must produce a team that moves on.

Result probabilitiesEstimates regulation outcome probabilities from team strength and context, then converts knockout ties into advance probabilities.
Score forecastUses a Poisson goals model to project expected goals and likely scorelines.
Similarity checkCompares fixtures with similar historical matches as a challenger model.
Expanded model benchRuns multinomial, negative-binomial, GAM, GBM, XGBoost, random-forest, SVM, and stepwise challengers before any promotion decision.

Guide

Forecast Dictionary

Short definitions for the terms that appear most often on the public forecast board and review pages.

Win probability

The model-estimated chance that a team wins the match when a regulation result can stand on its own.

Advance probability

The chance that a team moves on in a knockout match after regulation, extra time, or penalties.

Level after regulation

For knockout matches, the chance the score is tied after 90 minutes. It is not treated as a final draw.

Actual result probability

The probability the model assigned to what actually happened. Lower values show results the model did not expect well.

Upset check

A plain-language flag for completed matches where the actual result had a low model probability and deserves extra review.

Expected goals

The average score the model expects, not the exact score it promises.

Most likely score

The single scoreline with the highest probability in the score grid.

Prediction strength

A plain-language label that groups the top outcome probability into lean, medium, or strong.

Model agreement

How many of the baseline models point to the same winner, draw, or advancing team.

Brier score

A probability-quality score. Lower is better because it means the forecast probabilities stayed closer to reality.

RMSE

Root mean squared error. It punishes larger misses more than smaller ones. Lower is better.

MAE

Mean absolute error. It is the average size of the miss with no extra penalty for large errors. Lower is better.

Calibration

How closely the published probabilities match long-run results after enough matches have been graded.

Forecasts are probabilistic and should be used for research, discussion, and model review. They are not financial advice or guaranteed betting edges.
